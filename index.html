Act 3: The Vessel

The great doors of the throne room groan shut behind them. Stone columns rise like pillars of judgment. The flicker of torchlight dances across stained glass, and silence weighs heavier than steel.

ROWAN: You were never weak, brother. Not in the ways that matter.

RAPHAEL: Then why was I passed over like a dying dog? Why did Father strip me of everything?

ROWAN: Because of the prophecy. Because of what your Mother saw. You were her child... but also something more. She named you the vessel.

RAPHAEL: Vessel? Of what?

ROWAN: Mordrius. The god of death. She swore he would return... in you. Father feared it to be true.

RAPHAEL: And father believed her? Him—of all people?

ROWAN: I believe in you, Raphael. I still do.

SIEGWARD: My lords—enough. The court has ears. And worse.

A soft sound echoes: silk over stone. PONTIFF ORIANNA, spearhead of the Church of Celion, steps from the shadows, her hands folded, her voice a prayer and a curse.

ORIANNA: At last, the blood ripens. The soul stirs. The vessel wakes.

RAPHAEL: What are you doing Orianna?

ORIANNA: What I was always meant to do.

She raises her hands. Sigils burn into the air. The torches dim, snuffed by unseen breath. A deep rumble shakes the walls. Shadows coil, forming wings. The Black Dragon watches from beyond the veil.

ROWAN: Orianna—stop this madness!

ORIANNA: You would deny Dreigard its salvation?

RAPHAEL: I am no god. I am no vessel.

ORIANNA: Then why do the runes obey you? Why does death cling to you like breath?

The heroes draw steel. Orianna’s form unravels—no longer woman, but wyrm, her shape unraveling into smoke and flame. As the ground cracks and light shatters, Siegward stands resolute, blade raised, between the dragon and the crown.
